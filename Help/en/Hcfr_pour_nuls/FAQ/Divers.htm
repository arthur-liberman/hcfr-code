    1. What is offset, what is gain ?

      Offset and gain allow calibration on each primary colors, to have a neutral gray on all gray scale (from black to white).

      The offset acts on dark colors, and gain acts on bright colors. The gain is the       contrast, the white level of a single primary color. And offset is its brightness, the black level.

      So, when linearizing RGB levels to obtain a regular color temperature, gain will act on the last gray levels, and offset on the first levels.

      Practically, you start calibration using gain settings, then offset settings, and then cycle until result is perfect.

       

    2. Calibration files, a few answers

      Without calibration file, D65 calibration cannot be guaranteed. But all other operations can be done, and it's already nice work. You can perform all necessary measures for brightness, contrast, gamma, and component balance settings on all gray levels, which is the main part of calibration result. And with your eyes, you can approach D65 white (it's relatively easy to see if gray is bluish or greenish...)

      Even without calibration file, you can calibrate a projector in a very satisfying way, with results visibly superior to what can be done without instrument.

      The goal is to calibrate a projector to ensure white is D65 target point on CIE diagram. This point is a very unique color, but this color can be obtained with an infinite number of different light spectra. It's quite simple: D65 is identical for human eye, whichever light spectrum used to make it. For the sensor measuring red, green and blue (parts of light spectrum), two different D65 spectra will give two different sets of red/green/blue values. Because the sensor does not measure exactly the same thing than our eyes, the important thing is not to have a precise color from the sensor point of view, but from our eyes point of view.

      Basically, the calibration file building process consists in this: take a projector from a defined brand and model, do what is necessary to ensure it displays exactly D65 (ie: calibrate the projector), and look what HCFR probe measures for red, green and blue for this particular D65 color. The calibration file is the ratio between values returned by the sensor, which are characteristic of D65 light spectrum for this particular projector model. It can be then used as a target in HCFR colorimeter software, and is suitable for any exemplary of the same projector model.

      Calibration file does not depend on probe position.
